# Difficult Scenarios

Handling lowball offers, exploding offers, competing offers, "this is our final offer" claims, pay cuts, and entry-level negotiation challenges.

## Key Concepts

- A lowball offer is not necessarily an insult -- it may be a test to see if you negotiate, or it may reflect a genuine budget constraint
- Exploding offers (less than 48 hours) are a pressure tactic -- most companies will extend the deadline if you ask
- "Final offer" is rarely final -- it means "final at this level of approval"; you can often escalate to someone with more authority
- Competing offers are the single most powerful lever in negotiation -- even a verbal offer from another company counts
- Entry-level candidates have less leverage but can still negotiate on start date, team placement, and signing bonus
- A pay cut to join a "better" company can make long-term sense but should be a deliberate calculation, not a default

## Numbers & Benchmarks

- Lowball definition: 20%+ below market median for the role and level in that geography
- Reasonable offer response deadline: 5-7 business days; anything under 3 days is an aggressive timeline
- Average counteroffer acceptance from current employer: 50-70% initially accept, but 50-80% of those leave within 12 months
- Pay cut to join a top-tier company: 10-20% is common; more than 30% should be questioned unless the role is a unique career accelerator
- Entry-level negotiation success rate: 60-70% of employers expect some negotiation even for new graduates
- Stating a competing offer typically yields a 10-20% improvement over the original offer at most companies

## Common Pitfalls

- Taking an exploding offer at face value -- always ask for an extension with a specific reason ("I am waiting to complete another interview process")
- Bluffing about competing offers -- recruiters talk to each other and may verify; only reference real offers
- Accepting "final offer" without asking what it would take to go higher or asking to speak with the hiring manager's manager
- Negotiating entry-level salary by comparing to mid-level market data -- use new-graduate benchmarks
- Taking a pay cut without a clear ROI timeline (e.g., "I will recoup this within 18 months through the title bump")
- Burning bridges when walking away from a lowball -- the recruiter may resurface with a better offer or role later

## Scripts & Talking Points

- "I appreciate the offer, but the compensation is significantly below what I was expecting based on my research and conversations with peers. Is there room to get closer to $X?"
- "The deadline is quite tight. I want to make a thoughtful decision. Could we extend the deadline to [date] so I have time to properly evaluate?"
- "I understand this is your final offer at this level. Who would be the right person to discuss whether there is additional budget available?"
- "I have another opportunity that is offering $X total compensation. I would prefer to join your team because of [specific reason]. Can we close the gap?"
- "As a recent graduate, I am excited about this opportunity. I was hoping we could discuss the starting salary -- I had targeted $X based on my research. Even a small adjustment would help me feel confident in my decision."
