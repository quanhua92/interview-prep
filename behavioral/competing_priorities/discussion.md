# Handling Competing Priorities

## What Interviewers Look For
- Whether you proactively surface conflicts between priorities instead of silently dropping work
- Evidence that you use a structured framework for prioritization (impact, urgency, stakeholder value)
- Your ability to have transparent conversations with managers and stakeholders about trade-offs
- Whether you can say "no" or "not now" diplomatically while preserving the relationship

## STAR Framework Refresher
| Letter | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Set the scene in 1-2 sentences |
| T | Task | What was your responsibility or goal? |
| A | Action | What specific steps did YOU take? (use "I", not "we") |
| R | Result | What was the outcome? (quantify if possible) |

## Common Mistakes
- Saying yes to everything and then silently failing to deliver on some commitments
- Not pushing back when priorities are clearly unrealistic -- interviewers want to see healthy assertiveness
- Poor communication about trade-offs: stakeholders should know what they are giving up
- Prioritizing based on who asked loudest rather than what delivers the most value

## Story Mining Prompts
Think about these situations from your experience:
- Two managers or stakeholders gave you conflicting priorities and you had to navigate between them
- A feature deadline vs. tech debt -- delivering the feature meant accumulating more debt
- A customer escalation that conflicted with planned work you had committed to
- A personal growth project or initiative that competed with your team's deliverables
- Multiple projects in flight where dropping any one of them would upset a stakeholder

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "I had too much to do, so I just worked extra hours and got it all done."

This is bad because it shows no prioritization, no stakeholder communication, and treats overwork as a sustainable strategy.

**Good:** "I was assigned to three projects simultaneously: a customer-facing feature for the sales team, a backend performance investigation, and a security audit. I estimated effort for all three and realized there was no way to deliver quality work on all of them by the deadlines. I created a priority matrix showing business impact and deadline sensitivity, and brought it to my manager. We agreed the security audit was non-negotiable due to a compliance deadline, the sales feature was high-visibility but could be scoped down to the core workflow, and the performance investigation could move to next sprint. I communicated the revised plan to all three stakeholders. The security audit passed, the scoped-down feature shipped on time, and the performance investigation was picked up the following sprint."

## Variations to Prepare
- "Tell me about a time you had to juggle multiple competing priorities."
- "Describe a situation where you had to push back on a request."
- "Give me an example of how you decide what to work on when everything seems urgent."
- "How do you handle it when two stakeholders have conflicting priorities?"
