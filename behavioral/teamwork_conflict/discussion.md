# Conflict with a Teammate

## What Interviewers Look For
- Whether you can disagree respectfully without damaging the relationship
- Your ability to separate the issue from the person and keep things professional
- Evidence that you seek to understand the other person's perspective before pushing your own
- A pattern of finding solutions that both parties can commit to, not just "winning" the argument

## STAR Framework Refresher
| Letter | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Set the scene in 1-2 sentences |
| T | Task | What was your responsibility or goal? |
| A | Action | What specific steps did YOU take? (use "I", not "we") |
| R | Result | What was the outcome? (quantify if possible) |

## Common Mistakes
- Blaming the other person or framing yourself as the sole reasonable party in the conflict
- Describing a "conflict" where you just politely agreed with everything -- that avoids the question
- Skipping over HOW you resolved it and jumping straight to "we figured it out"
- Framing compromise as a loss instead of a deliberate, thoughtful decision

## Story Mining Prompts
Think about these situations from your experience:
- A time you and a peer disagreed on a technical approach (framework choice, architecture pattern, coding style) and had to align
- A personality clash with a teammate that made collaboration difficult -- how did you bridge the gap?
- Competing priorities with a peer where both of you felt your work was more important
- A disagreement about scope, timeline, or design that escalated beyond a quick chat
- Working with someone whose communication style was fundamentally different from yours

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "A teammate and I disagreed on the tech stack. We talked about it and decided to go with my approach. It worked out fine."

This is bad because it shows no empathy, no process for resolving the disagreement, and frames the outcome as "I won."

**Good:** "A teammate and I disagreed on whether to use a monolith or microservices for a new service. I initially favored microservices because of scalability, but instead of pushing my preference, I set up a 30-minute whiteboard session where we each wrote down our constraints. I realized his concern about operational complexity was valid given our small DevOps team. We agreed on a modular monolith that could be split later. That architecture still serves us today with zero migration cost so far, and we shipped two weeks faster than the original estimate."

## Variations to Prepare
- "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a coworker."
- "Describe a situation where you had to work with someone difficult."
- "Give me an example of how you handle interpersonal conflict at work."
- "Have you ever had to push back on a teammate's idea? How did you handle it?"
