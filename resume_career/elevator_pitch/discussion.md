# Elevator Pitch

## Overview
A concise 30-60 second self-introduction that answers "Tell me about yourself" by covering who you are, what you do, your key strength, and what you are looking for -- all in a natural conversational tone.

## Best Practices
- Aim for 60-90 words total, which takes about 30-45 seconds to speak at a normal pace
- Lead with your current role and years of experience -- give the listener an immediate anchor
- Include one or two specific achievements with numbers to establish credibility quickly
- Tailor the pitch to your audience -- emphasize different strengths for a hiring manager vs. a networking event
- End with a forward-looking statement about what you are looking for or what excites you next
- Practice until it sounds natural, not rehearsed -- avoid sounding like you are reading a script
- Do not recite your resume verbatim -- the pitch should complement, not duplicate, your written materials

## Structure / Framework
Use this four-part formula:
1. **Current Identity** (1 sentence): "I am a [role] with [X] years of experience [in/doing Y]."
2. **Key Strength or Specialty** (1-2 sentences): What distinguishes you from others with a similar title.
3. **Proof Point** (1 sentence): One quantified achievement that demonstrates your strength.
4. **Forward Hook** (1 sentence): What you are looking for or what draws you to this opportunity.

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "Hi, I'm Alex. I've been a software engineer for about five years. I've worked at a few different companies doing mostly backend stuff with Java and Python. I'm really passionate about coding and I'm looking for a new role where I can grow and make an impact."

This is bad because it is vague, uses filler language ("mostly," "stuff," "really passionate"), has no quantified achievements, and gives the listener nothing specific to remember.

**Good:** "I'm a backend engineer with five years of experience building distributed systems at scale. I specialize in designing event-driven architectures -- at my last role I built a real-time data pipeline processing two million events per minute, which cut our analytics latency from 15 minutes to under 30 seconds. I'm now looking for a role where I can take on more system design ownership and help a team scale their infrastructure."

This is good because it immediately establishes identity and specialty, backs it up with a specific and quantified achievement, and ends with a clear statement of what the person is seeking.

## Common Mistakes
- Starting with a long personal history ("I grew up in..." or "I always loved computers since...") -- interviewers want your professional identity, not a biography
- Overloading the pitch with too many accomplishments -- one strong proof point beats three weak ones
- Using jargon or buzzwords without context ("I leverage synergies to drive paradigm shifts")
- Ending on a weak note like "That's pretty much it" or "I'm open to anything" -- this signals lack of direction
- Memorizing it word-for-word so it sounds robotic -- memorize the structure, not the script
- Making it too long -- if you cannot say it comfortably in under 60 seconds, trim it
