# Questions for Interviewer

## Overview
Strategic questions to ask at the end of interviews that demonstrate curiosity, critical thinking, and genuine interest -- while also helping you evaluate whether the role and company are a good fit.

## Best Practices
- Always prepare 5-8 questions so you have options depending on what was covered during the interview
- Never ask questions that could be answered by reading the company website or job description
- Tailor your questions to the interviewer's role -- ask the hiring manager about strategy, the engineer about technical decisions, the recruiter about process
- Listen carefully during the interview and ask follow-up questions based on what was discussed -- this shows attentiveness better than any prepared question
- Avoid asking about salary, benefits, or PTO in the first interview unless the interviewer brings it up
- Ask at least one question in every interview -- saying "I think you covered everything" signals low engagement
- Save your strongest question for last -- interviewers often remember the final exchange most clearly

## Question Categories and What They Signal

### Questions About the Work (signals technical curiosity)
- "What is the most technically challenging problem the team is facing right now?"
- "How does the team make architecture decisions? Is there a formal process or is it more collaborative?"
- "What does the deployment pipeline look like, and how often does the team ship to production?"

### Questions About the Team (signals collaboration awareness)
- "How is work distributed among team members -- do people own specific areas or is it more fluid?"
- "How does the team handle code reviews? What is the typical turnaround time?"
- "Can you describe the dynamic between engineering and product? How are disagreements about scope resolved?"

### Questions About Growth (signals ambition and long-term thinking)
- "What does the career progression look like for someone in this role? Are there examples of people who have grown here?"
- "What skills or experiences would make someone exceptionally successful in this role after the first year?"
- "How does the company support professional development -- conferences, learning budgets, internal mobility?"

### Questions About Culture (signals self-awareness about fit)
- "What is something about working here that surprised you when you first joined?"
- "How does the team handle situations where a deadline is at risk?"
- "What does the onboarding process look like for new engineers on this team?"

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "So, what does the company do?" or "What will my day-to-day look like?"

These are bad because they show a lack of basic research. The first question is answered on the company homepage, and the second should have been covered in the job description or earlier in the interview.

**Bad:** "Do you offer remote work? How much PTO do I get?"

These are not inherently bad questions, but asking them in the first round signals that your priorities are misaligned. Save these for the recruiter or final round.

**Good:** "You mentioned the team is migrating from a monolith to microservices. What has been the biggest unexpected challenge in that migration so far?"

This is good because it shows you were listening, demonstrates technical curiosity, and gives the interviewer a chance to share something real and specific.

## Common Mistakes
- Asking no questions at all or saying "You covered everything" -- this is one of the most common reasons candidates are rejected
- Asking questions only about perks and benefits in early rounds, which signals the wrong priorities
- Reading questions off a notepad without making eye contact -- prepare but do not script
- Asking the same generic question ("What is the culture like?") to every interviewer instead of tailoring
- Using questions to subtly boast ("What is the hardest problem on the team? I bet I could help with that") -- stay genuine
- Forgetting that questions are also an evaluation tool for you -- ask things you genuinely want to know
