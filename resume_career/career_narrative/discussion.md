# Career Narrative

## Overview
How to connect your professional experience into a coherent story that shows intentional progression, explains transitions, and highlights growth arcs -- giving interviewers a clear picture of who you are and where you are headed.

## Best Practices
- Identify a unifying thread across your roles -- it could be a technical domain, a type of problem you solve, or a skill you have deepened
- Frame each role as a deliberate step, not a random jump -- even lateral moves can show breadth that strengthens your profile
- Connect past experience to the role you are interviewing for by showing how each previous position built a skill you will use here
- Be honest about transitions but frame them positively -- "I moved to learn X" is better than "I left because I was bored"
- Prepare a two-minute version for interviews and a one-sentence version for networking events
- Show growth by contrasting where you started with where you are now, using concrete examples of expanding scope

## Structure / Framework
Use this three-act structure:
1. **Origin** (15-20 seconds): Where did you start and what initially drew you to this field?
2. **Progression** (60-90 seconds): What were the 2-3 pivotal roles or projects that shaped your career? For each, explain what you learned and how it led to the next step.
3. **Direction** (20-30 seconds): Where are you now, and what is the next chapter you want to write?

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "I started as a junior dev at Company A, then I moved to Company B, then I worked at Company C doing backend, and now I'm here."

This is bad because it is just a timeline with no story. It gives the interviewer no insight into why you made each move or what connects the experiences.

**Good:** "I started my career as a frontend engineer because I loved the immediacy of building interfaces people interact with. After two years, I took on a project that required me to build the backend API as well, and I realized I was more drawn to the data modeling and system design challenges. That led me to a backend-focused role at Company B, where I owned a service handling 50K requests per minute. Now, after four years on the backend, I'm looking for roles where I can combine both -- designing systems end to end, from the API down to the database."

This is good because it shows a clear arc from frontend to backend, explains the motivation behind each transition, demonstrates growth in scope, and naturally connects to what the candidate wants next.

## Common Mistakes
- Framing every job change as "I wanted a new challenge" -- it sounds evasive after the second use
- Skipping over shorter stints or roles you are not proud of -- gaps in the narrative raise more questions than brief, honest explanations
- Being too rigid about one linear story -- if your path was not linear, frame it as breadth that gives you a unique perspective
- Focusing only on titles and companies instead of what you actually did and learned at each stop
- Making the story entirely about technical growth without mentioning collaboration, leadership, or impact on others
