# Dealing with Failure

## What Interviewers Look For
- Whether you can talk about failure openly without deflecting or making excuses
- Evidence of genuine self-reflection and specific lessons learned (not platitudes)
- A track record of applying those lessons to subsequent situations
- Resilience -- did the failure demoralize you, or did it fuel improvement?

## STAR Framework Refresher
| Letter | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Set the scene in 1-2 sentences |
| T | Task | What was your responsibility or goal? |
| A | Action | What specific steps did YOU take? (use "I", not "we") |
| R | Result | What was the outcome? (quantify if possible) |

## Common Mistakes
- Choosing a "failure" that is actually a humblebrag (e.g., "I worked too hard and burned out")
- Giving a vague lesson like "I learned to communicate better" without a concrete behavior change
- Being excessively self-critical to the point where it undermines confidence in your competence
- Skipping the "what I did next" part -- interviewers want to see the bounce-back, not just the fall

## Story Mining Prompts
Think about these situations from your experience:
- A project that missed its deadline or shipped with significant issues
- A wrong technical decision you made (choosing the wrong tool, wrong architecture, wrong approach)
- A time you did not get a promotion, role, or opportunity you wanted
- Code you wrote that caused an outage, data loss, or significant customer impact
- A presentation, proposal, or initiative that was rejected or failed to gain traction

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "I once missed a deadline because we didn't have enough time. I learned to manage my time better."

This is bad because it externalizes the cause ("we didn't have enough time") and the lesson is a generic platitude with no specific action attached.

**Good:** "I led a database migration that I estimated would take two weekends. By the second weekend, we were only 40% done because I hadn't accounted for data format inconsistencies between regions. I had to work with the PM to negotiate a phased rollout over four weekends instead. Afterward, I created a migration playbook that included a data sampling step for detecting inconsistencies early. I've used that playbook for three subsequent migrations, and all three completed on schedule with zero downtime."

## Variations to Prepare
- "Tell me about a time you failed at something."
- "Describe a project that did not go as planned. What happened?"
- "Give me an example of a mistake that taught you an important lesson."
- "When is the last time you were wrong about something significant at work?"
