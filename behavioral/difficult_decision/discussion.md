# Making a Tough Technical Decision

## What Interviewers Look For
- Your ability to weigh trade-offs clearly and articulate a rationale, not just pick a side
- Evidence that you gathered input from the right people before deciding
- Whether you can commit to a decision even when information is incomplete
- How you communicate the decision and its implications to stakeholders afterward

## STAR Framework Refresher
| Letter | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Set the scene in 1-2 sentences |
| T | Task | What was your responsibility or goal? |
| A | Action | What specific steps did YOU take? (use "I", not "we") |
| R | Result | What was the outcome? (quantify if possible) |

## Common Mistakes
- Describing a decision with no real trade-off (e.g., "I chose the obviously better option")
- Getting stuck in analysis paralysis and never actually making the call
- Making the decision in isolation without consulting stakeholders or subject-matter experts
- Failing to explain the WHY behind the decision -- interviewers care about your reasoning process

## Story Mining Prompts
Think about these situations from your experience:
- Choosing a tech stack, framework, or library for a new project with competing options
- Deciding to deprecate an old system and planning the migration strategy
- A build vs. buy decision where both paths had significant pros and cons
- Prioritizing between two equally urgent and important tasks or projects
- Deciding whether to refactor a working system or build a replacement from scratch

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "We needed a new database, so I researched options and picked PostgreSQL because it's the best. It worked great."

This is bad because there is no trade-off analysis, no mention of alternatives considered, and no stakeholder involvement.

**Good:** "We needed to replace our aging search service. I evaluated Elasticsearch, Algolia, and Meilisearch against five criteria: latency, cost, ops burden, relevance quality, and team familiarity. Elasticsearch scored highest on relevance but would have required hiring a specialist. I presented a decision matrix to the team and recommended Meilisearch based on our constraints: we had two engineers available for ops and a $5k/month budget. Search latency improved by 60%, and we stayed under budget. Six months later, we have not needed to hire for that expertise."

## Variations to Prepare
- "Tell me about a difficult technical decision you've made."
- "Describe a time you had to choose between two imperfect options."
- "Give me an example of a time you made a decision with incomplete information."
- "How do you approach trade-off analysis when there's no clear right answer?"
