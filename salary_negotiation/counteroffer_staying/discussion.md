# Counteroffer & Staying

When to accept a counteroffer from your current employer, the hidden risks of staying, and how to resign professionally when you decide to leave.

## Key Concepts

- A counteroffer is a reactive retention tool, not a proactive reward for your value -- it means your employer was underpaying you
- Accepting a counteroffer addresses the symptom (compensation) but rarely fixes the root cause (why you started looking in the first place)
- Statistics consistently show that 50-80% of employees who accept counteroffers leave within 12 months
- Your employer now knows you are willing to leave, which can affect future project assignments, promotion decisions, and trust
- If you resign, do it professionally and in writing -- never burn bridges in the tech industry because reputations follow you
- The only good reason to accept a counteroffer is if the original reason for leaving was purely financial and the counteroffer comprehensively addresses it

## Numbers & Benchmarks

- Average counteroffer raise: 10-20% above your current salary (sometimes more if the market has moved significantly)
- Average external offer raise: 15-30% above current salary for same-level moves; 25-50% for level-up moves
- Employee retention after counteroffer: 50% leave within 6 months, 70-80% within 12 months (multiple industry surveys)
- Time to replace an employee: typically 3-6 months for engineering roles, costing 1.5-2x the employee's salary in recruitment and onboarding
- Notice period: 2 weeks standard in the US; some companies request 4 weeks; executive roles may require 60-90 days
- If you are in the top 10% of performers, your employer has strong incentive to counter -- replacement cost for strong talent is even higher

## Common Pitfalls

- Accepting a counteroffer because it feels flattering -- your employer is making a business decision, not a personal one
- Using a counteroffer as a bluff to get a raise -- this almost always backfires if you end up staying
- Not having a written resignation letter -- verbal resignations lead to miscommunication and complications
- Badmouthing your current employer during the exit process -- even if justified, it serves no purpose and can damage your reputation
- Forgetting to ask about clawback provisions on signing bonuses or relocation if you leave before a certain date
- Not requesting a reference or LinkedIn recommendation before your last day, when relationships are still warm

## Scripts & Talking Points

- "I want to be upfront with you. I have received an offer from another company, and I have decided to accept it. My last day will be [date]. I am grateful for the opportunities here and want to ensure a smooth transition."
- "I appreciate the counteroffer and I understand why you want me to stay. However, my decision to leave was not solely about compensation. The new role offers [specific growth opportunity/project/technology] that aligns with my long-term career goals."
- "Before I make my final decision, can we discuss what a counteroffer would look like? I want to be transparent about where I stand."
- "I am happy to help with the transition. Let me put together a knowledge transfer document and ensure my projects are handed off smoothly before my last day."
- "I want to make sure I understand the clawback provisions on my signing bonus. Can you clarify what I would owe if I leave before [date]?"
