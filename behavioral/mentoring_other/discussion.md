# Mentoring a Teammate

## What Interviewers Look For
- Whether you can teach complex concepts without being condescending or oversimplifying
- Evidence of adapting your approach to the mentee's learning style and experience level
- Patience -- mentoring is not about doing it for them, it is about guiding them to do it themselves
- Whether you invest in mentoring beyond just answering occasional questions (structured, intentional effort)

## STAR Framework Refresher
| Letter | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Set the scene in 1-2 sentences |
| T | Task | What was your responsibility or goal? |
| A | Action | What specific steps did YOU take? (use "I", not "we") |
| R | Result | What was the outcome? (quantify if possible) |

## Common Mistakes
- Doing the work for the mentee instead of guiding them -- this creates dependency, not growth
- Being condescending or impatient, especially with junior engineers who ask basic questions
- Using a one-size-fits-all approach without assessing the mentee's current skill level and goals
- Claiming credit for the mentee's accomplishments or framing it as a story about yourself

## Story Mining Prompts
Think about these situations from your experience:
- Onboarding a new team member and helping them ramp up faster than the average
- Helping a struggling teammate improve in a specific area (coding, testing, communication)
- Teaching a new technology, framework, or process to your team (e.g., introducing Kubernetes, TDD, etc.)
- Creating documentation, runbooks, or training materials that benefited the broader team
- Informally coaching a peer through a challenging task or career decision

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "A new joiner didn't know our codebase, so I helped them by pair programming with them for a few days."

This is bad because it lacks structure, shows no adaptation to the mentee's needs, and has no measurable outcome.

**Good:** "A new mid-level engineer joined our team and was struggling with our distributed system's debugging workflow. Instead of answering questions ad hoc, I created a 'debugging walkthrough' document with three real production incidents we had solved. I also set up twice-weekly 30-minute pair debugging sessions where I would let them drive and only ask guiding questions. After four weeks, they independently resolved a production issue that had stumped two other engineers. They later told me the structured walkthrough was the single most useful resource during their onboarding."

## Variations to Prepare
- "Tell me about a time you mentored someone."
- "Describe a situation where you helped a teammate grow."
- "Give me an example of how you've contributed to the growth of others on your team."
- "How do you approach teaching a complex technical concept to someone less experienced?"
