# Responding to Critical Feedback

## What Interviewers Look For
- Whether you listen to feedback without immediately getting defensive or making excuses
- Evidence that you process the feedback and take specific, measurable action on it
- Humility -- can you acknowledge a genuine area of improvement without self-flagellation?
- Follow-through -- the interviewer wants to see that the feedback changed your behavior over time

## STAR Framework Refresher
| Letter | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| S | Situation | Set the scene in 1-2 sentences |
| T | Task | What was your responsibility or goal? |
| A | Action | What specific steps did YOU take? (use "I", not "we") |
| R | Result | What was the outcome? (quantify if possible) |

## Common Mistakes
- Getting defensive in the story ("I understood their point, but actually I was right because...")
- Dismissing the feedback as subjective or unfair, even if it stung
- Over-apologizing in a way that undermines your credibility ("I'm terrible at X and always have been")
- Describing feedback you received but not explaining what you DID differently afterward

## Story Mining Prompts
Think about these situations from your experience:
- A negative performance review or piece of critical feedback from your manager
- A code review that called out a recurring pattern or significant issue in your work
- A time your manager told you that you needed to improve in a specific area (communication, estimation, ownership)
- A peer or teammate who told you something honest and hard to hear about how you work
- Feedback from a customer or stakeholder that changed how you approached your work

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Bad:** "My manager told me my code reviews were too harsh. I thanked her for the feedback and tried to be nicer."

This is bad because it has no specifics about what changed, no self-reflection, and no measurable outcome.

**Good:** "In a 1:1, my manager said my code review comments came across as dismissive -- I would write things like 'this is wrong' without explaining why. It was hard to hear because I thought I was being efficient. I asked two teammates whose reviews I respected if they'd noticed the same thing, and they confirmed it. I started rewriting my comments in a 'situation-impact-suggestion' format and began every review with something I appreciated about the PR. Three months later, my manager told me that two junior engineers specifically mentioned my reviews as helpful in their feedback survey."

## Variations to Prepare
- "Tell me about a time you received critical feedback. How did you respond?"
- "Describe a situation where someone told you something about your work that was hard to hear."
- "Give me an example of how you've grown from feedback."
- "What is the most useful piece of feedback you've received, and what did you do with it?"
