# Cross-Border Remote Salary Negotiation

Negotiating compensation as a Vietnam-based engineer for US and APAC companies. Remote-first and cross-border employment introduces challenges that traditional salary negotiation guides don't cover: geographic pay models, employment classification, currency risk, benefits gaps, and the unique leverage dynamics of the global talent market.

## Key Concepts

- "Cost of labor" is not "cost of living" -- companies pay based on what they can hire for in your region, not what your personal expenses are. Never argue COL in a negotiation; argue value.
- Remote compensation models vary wildly between companies -- two identical roles at two remote-first companies can have 2-3x pay difference purely because of how they model geographic pay.
- Your employment structure (employee via EOR vs. contractor) determines your tax obligations, benefits access, equity eligibility, and job security -- always clarify this before negotiating numbers.
- Vietnam is a rising-cost market -- the "$2K/month senior developer" era is over. HCMC senior engineers at international companies now command $3,000-$6,000+/month, and remote roles for US companies often exceed local market rates by 30-50%.
- Benefits don't transfer across borders -- health insurance, 401(k) matching, stock purchase plans, and other perks US employees take for granted often don't exist for Vietnam-based workers. Factor this gap into your total comp calculation.
- Currency risk is a hidden tax -- being paid in USD protects against VND inflation but exposes you to VND appreciation. A 5% move in USD/VND on a $50K salary is $2,500/year.
- The global market is your leverage -- remote work means your competition is not just other engineers in Vietnam, but engineers globally. This is both opportunity (access to higher-paying roles) and threat (global competition for the same roles).

## Common Pitfalls

- Accepting a rate based on "Vietnam local market" without considering that the role is paying for global competition -- US companies often budget more than local rates for equivalent work.
- Not clarifying employment structure until after signing -- EOR employee, direct entity employee, and contractor have fundamentally different implications for taxes, benefits, and equity.
- Ignoring currency risk -- a USD salary looks great until VND strengthens. Negotiate USD denomination and understand the exchange rate mechanism.
- Not factoring in self-paid benefits -- if the company doesn't provide health insurance, good coverage in Vietnam costs $1,000-$3,000/year out of pocket. If they don't match retirement, you lose 3-6% of salary in deferred compensation.
- Negotiating only base salary without considering allowances -- equipment setup, internet, coworking, learning budget, and annual home office stipend can add $2,000-$5,000/year to effective comp.
- Not asking about payment frequency and method -- wire transfer fees and PayPal fees can eat 2-5% of your salary if you don't negotiate who absorbs them.
- Overvaluing equity from early-stage startups -- the same 50-70% haircut rule applies, but non-US employees may face additional illiquidity and complexity in exercising options or selling shares.

## Scripts & Talking Points

- "Before we discuss numbers, I'd like to understand the compensation structure for remote employees in my region. Is there a geographic pay adjustment, and if so, how is it calculated?"
- "I've researched comparable remote roles for US companies hiring in Vietnam at this level, and the range I'm seeing is $X-$Y annually. How does that compare to your budget for this position?"
- "Could you clarify the employment structure? Will I be on a local entity through an EOR, a direct entity, or is this a contractor arrangement? I'd like to understand the benefits and tax implications."
- "What currency will compensation be paid in, and how is the exchange rate handled? Is there any FX protection if I'm paid in a currency other than my local one?"
- "What benefits are available for employees in my region? I'd like to understand the health insurance coverage, equipment allowance, learning budget, and whether equity participation is available."
- "I notice the rate is below the US band for this level. Given the overlap in working hours and the value I can deliver in real-time collaboration, is there room to come closer to the global rate?"
- "Beyond base salary, I'd like to discuss the total package. Can we look at equipment setup, annual learning budget, home office stipend, and whether there's flexibility on PTO?"
- "I'd prefer not to convert my current VND salary to USD for this discussion. I'm more interested in what's appropriate for this role based on its scope and the global talent market."

## Remote Compensation Models: How Companies Pay Remote Workers

Understanding which model a company uses is the single most important piece of information before any negotiation. It determines the ceiling of what you can realistically achieve.

| Model | How It Works | Pay Level (Vietnam) | Examples | Negotiation Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Location-agnostic** | Same pay regardless of geography | Highest -- often 80-100% of US rate | Basecamp, Automattic, Wildbit, Help Scout, some YC startups | Anchor to US market rate; argue value, not location |
| **Geo-tiered (3-5 tiers)** | Pay adjusted by region tier | Medium-High -- 50-80% of US rate | GitLab, Stripe, Notion, many US tech companies | Ask which tier Vietnam falls in; negotiate tier assignment |
| **Country-band** | Fixed band per country | Medium -- tied to local market + premium | Many APAC companies (Singapore, Japan, Australia employers) | Compare to other US companies hiring in Vietnam |
| **Local entity rate** | Pay aligned to local office market | Lowest -- same as in-country hires | Companies with Vietnam offices (Grab, VNG, NAB) | Negotiate remote premium since you're not using office resources |
| **EOR with geo-adjustment** | Local employment via EOR with pay bands | Varies -- depends on company's global model | Deel/Remote.com clients | Understand the EOR doesn't set pay; the hiring company does |

**Key insight:** You cannot change the model, but you can influence which tier you're assigned to and argue for exceptions. A senior engineer who covers US hours and delivers real-time collaboration is worth more than the same engineer working asynchronously on Vietnam time.

## Employment Structure: EOR vs. Direct Entity vs. Contractor

How you're classified affects everything beyond base salary. Clarify this before discussing numbers.

### Employee via EOR (Employer of Record)

**What it is:** The company hires you through a third-party platform (Deel, Remote.com, Oyster, Papaya Global) that acts as your legal employer in Vietnam. You are a full employee under Vietnamese labor law.

**Pros:**
- Full labor protections under Vietnamese law (social insurance, severance, paid leave)
- Employer handles tax withholding, social insurance contributions, and compliance
- Structured benefits through the EOR platform
- Cleaner employment history (no gaps in social insurance)

**Cons:**
- EOR benefits are typically basic -- social insurance + optional health insurance top-up
- Equity/RSU participation may be limited or structured differently
- The EOR platform sits between you and the hiring company for HR matters
- Some EORs charge employees for premium benefit add-ons

**Negotiation focus:** Base salary, learning budget, equipment allowance, and PTO. Equity access is harder to negotiate through EOR structures.

### Direct Entity Employee

**What it is:** The hiring company has a legal entity in Vietnam and employs you directly. Common for companies with existing Vietnam offices (Grab, NAB, FPT, VNG).

**Pros:**
- Full local labor protections
- Benefits may be more comprehensive (health insurance, meal allowance, annual health check)
- 13th month is standard
- Equity/stock options may be available through the local entity or parent company
- Career mobility within the local office

**Cons:**
- Pay aligned to local market rates, not global rates
- Less flexibility to negotiate above local bands
- Subject to local promotion cycles and office politics

**Negotiation focus:** Level/title, promotion path, and whether you can access parent company equity programs.

### Contractor (B2B)

**What it is:** You operate as an independent contractor or through your own company. Common for US startups that don't want EOR costs.

**Pros:**
- Higher gross pay possible (20-40% above employee rate to cover self-employment costs)
- No social insurance deduction from salary
- Flexibility on hours and scope (sometimes)
- Can deduct business expenses (equipment, internet, coworking)

**Cons:**
- No labor protections -- no severance, no paid leave, no social insurance
- You handle your own tax (5-35% PIT on business income, 1-5% VAT depending on structure)
- No benefits unless explicitly negotiated
- No unemployment protection -- contract can end with minimal notice
- Visa/immigration complications if you ever need to visit the hiring company's office
- Some platforms (Upwork, Toptal) take 10-20% of your earnings

**Negotiation focus:** Gross rate must cover self-employment tax, self-funded benefits, and lack of job security. Negotiate explicitly for equipment, learning budget, and contract terms (minimum notice period, scope definition).

### Household Business Registration (Hộ Kinh Doanh Cá Thể)

**What it is:** Register as an individual business household (HKD) at your local district office. This is a legal business entity recognized under Vietnamese law -- simpler than an LLC but with a tax identification number, business registration certificate, and the ability to open a dedicated business bank account.

**Why it matters for remote workers:** Many Vietnam-based freelancers working for US/APAC companies use this structure. It provides tax compliance, a business bank account for receiving foreign currency, and a legal framework for the relationship. Some US companies require a business entity and will not pay individual personal bank accounts.

**Tax structure (effective January 1, 2026):**

| Revenue Level | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|
| Up to VND 500 million/year (~$19,500 USD) | **Tax-exempt** -- no VAT, no PIT |
| Over VND 500 million/year -- IT services (freelancing, consulting, development) | **7% flat** (5% VAT + 2% PIT on gross revenue) |

Note: Decree 141/2026/NĐ-CP further raised the exemption threshold to VND 1 billion/year (~$39,000 USD) in some interpretations. The VND 500 million threshold is confirmed per Decision 3389/QĐ-BTC. The higher threshold may apply -- consult a local tax advisor for the latest interpretation.

The 7% rate applies to the "service sector" (dịch vụ) classification under Appendix I of Circular 40/2021/TT-BTC. This is the category most IT freelancers and remote workers fall under. The 0% VAT "software production/export" rate is a CIT incentive designed for registered companies, not individual household businesses -- it is very difficult for HKDs to qualify. The 2% total rate is theoretically possible under the "production sector" classification but requires meeting criteria that are impractical for most freelance arrangements. Plan for 7%.

**Key advantage over personal employment income:** The flat 7% on gross revenue is still significantly lower than the progressive 5-35% PIT on personal employment income. At $60K/year, you'd pay roughly $4,200 in HKD tax vs. $6,000-$15,000 in personal PIT. However, you must factor in the lack of benefits (no social insurance, no health insurance, no paid leave).

**Registration requirements:**
- Register at the district-level Economic Division (Phòng Kinh tế) or via the National Business Registration Portal
- Provide: ID card, business address, business scope (IT services / software development)
- Registration fee: nominal (typically VND 100,000-300,000)
- Processing time: 3-5 business days
- You receive: Business Registration Certificate (Giấy phép kinh doanh) and Tax Code (Mã số thuế)
- Annual license fee (lệ phí môn bài): **Abolished** as of January 1, 2026

**Bank account:**
- Open a business bank account in the name of the household business (e.g., "HKD Nguyen Van A")
- Can receive USD via SWIFT/wire transfer from foreign clients
- Banks will require: HKD Registration Certificate, Tax Code, and a copy of your service contract with the foreign client
- Recommended banks: Techcombank, VIB, ACB (good digital banking and easy USD-to-VND conversion)
- To spend in Vietnam: sell USD to the bank for VND. Can hold USD in the account to hedge currency risk.
- Can use USD to pay international business expenses (AWS, GitHub subscriptions) with valid invoices

**Limitations vs. LLC (Công ty TNHH):**
- No limited liability -- personal assets are not protected
- Cannot hire more than 10 employees (10 for individuals, 50 if registered as a household group)
- Cannot issue VAT invoices under VND 500 million revenue
- May face scrutiny if revenue grows significantly (some companies prefer contracting with an LLC)
- Cannot raise capital or issue shares

**Negotiation focus:** When using HKD structure, your gross rate can be lower than an employee rate and still yield higher net income because of the 7% flat tax advantage. However, you must factor in the lack of benefits, no social insurance, and the administrative burden of registration, quarterly tax filing, and annual finalization.

## Equity for Non-US Employees

Equity compensation is one of the largest gaps between US and Vietnam-based remote employees.

**RSUs:** Many US companies issue RSUs only to US employees or employees in specific countries. Vietnam-based employees may be excluded from RSU programs entirely, or receive a cash bonus in lieu.

**Stock options:** More commonly offered to non-US employees, but exercise and tax mechanics differ:
- Options are typically granted under a US parent company plan
- Exercise may require a US brokerage account (e.g., E*Trade, Schwab)
- Tax treatment: Vietnam taxes stock option gains as employment income at exercise or vesting
- 83(b) election is a US tax concept -- not available to non-US taxpayers
- Liquidity is often more limited for non-US employees (restricted trading windows, additional compliance)
- For private companies: exercise costs, tax obligations, and illiquidity are all amplified for non-US employees

**What to ask:**
- "Is equity available for employees in Vietnam? If so, is it RSUs or stock options?"
- "How is equity taxed in Vietnam? Does the company provide tax support or gross-up for equity income?"
- "If equity isn't available, is there a cash bonus or profit-sharing program that replaces it?"
- "What is the exercise window for options? Is there early exercise available, and what are the tax implications in Vietnam?"

**Practical rule:** If a company offers $50K base + $20K RSU to a US employee and offers you $50K base with no equity, you're not getting the same offer. Negotiate the gap -- either push for equity access or ask for additional base/bonus to compensate.

## Currency & Payment Negotiation

### USD-Denominated Contracts

Most US companies pay Vietnam-based remote workers in USD. This is advantageous when VND is weakening (your USD buys more locally) and disadvantageous when VND is strengthening (your purchasing power decreases).

**What to negotiate:**
- Fixed USD amount with no FX conversion -- most common and cleanest
- Payment method: Wise (formerly TransferWise) is the gold standard -- fast, low fees (~$1-$5), good exchange rate. Bank wire is acceptable but slower ($15-$50 per transfer). Avoid PayPal if possible (~4.5% total fees on international receiving).
- Payment frequency: monthly is standard. Some companies pay semi-monthly, which smooths cash flow.
- Who absorbs transfer fees: negotiate that the employer covers all payment processing fees

### Non-USD Contracts

APAC companies (Singapore, Japan, Australia) may pay in local currency:

| Currency | Typical for | Exchange Rate Risk | Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| SGD | Singapore-based companies | Moderate | Relatively stable against USD; good purchasing power |
| JPY | Japan-based companies | High | JPY has been weak; if paid in JPY and converted to VND, rate matters |
| AUD | Australia-based companies | Moderate | AUD fluctuates; clarify if rate is fixed or floating |
| USD | US companies (most common) | Low for Vietnam | Best option for Vietnam-based workers |

**What to negotiate:**
- Always ask: "What currency is compensation paid in?"
- If non-USD: "How is the exchange rate determined? Is it fixed at a specific rate, or does it float with the market?"
- Push for USD denomination when possible -- it's the most stable reference currency for Vietnam
- If the company insists on local currency, negotiate a higher base to hedge FX risk (typically 5-10% buffer)

### The Hidden Cost of Payment Methods

| Method | Receiving Fee | Speed | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| HKD business bank account (SWIFT) | $15-$30 per transfer | 2-5 business days | Tax-compliant contractor receiving USD from foreign clients |
| Wise (Business) | $1-$5 | 1-2 business days | Standard for most remote workers |
| Direct bank wire | $15-$50 | 2-5 business days | Large companies with banking relationships |
| PayPal | ~2.99% + 1.50% international + fixed fee (~4.5% total) | Instant to 2 days | Avoid if possible |
| Payoneer | 1-3% | 2-3 business days | Some APAC companies prefer this |
| Crypto | Variable | Minutes to hours | Uncommon; only if you request it |

## Benefits & Allowances: What to Negotiate Beyond Salary

When benefits don't transfer across borders, you need to negotiate for the equivalent value.

### Must-Negotiate Items

**Equipment setup (one-time):** $1,000-$3,000 for laptop, monitor, keyboard, mouse, and peripherals. Most US remote companies provide this; if they don't, negotiate a one-time equipment stipend.

**Internet allowance (monthly):** $20-$50/month for high-speed internet suitable for video calls and development work. Some companies include this; others don't.

**Learning & development budget (annual):** $500-$3,000/year for courses, certifications, conferences, and books. This is one of the most commonly available allowances for remote workers and directly invests in your career.

**Home office allowance (annual or one-time):** $500-$2,000 for desk, chair, lighting, and other workspace improvements. Often offered as a one-time setup payment.

### Nice-to-Have Items

**Coworking stipend:** $100-$300/month for coworking space. Less common but increasingly offered by remote-first companies.

**PTO:** US companies typically offer 15-25 days. Vietnam statutory minimum is 12 days + public holidays. Negotiate for at least 15 days, ideally 20+ for senior roles.

**Health insurance top-up:** Beyond mandatory social insurance, good private health insurance in Vietnam costs $1,000-$3,000/year. Negotiate for the company to cover or subsidize this.

**Sabbatical:** After 3-5 years, some companies offer 4-8 weeks paid sabbatical. Rare for remote workers but worth asking about at senior levels.

**Relocation assistance:** If the company ever wants you to visit, negotiate who covers flights, accommodation, and visa costs upfront.

### Script for Negotiating Benefits

"Beyond base salary, I'd like to discuss the total package. Specifically, I'm looking for:
- Equipment setup support for my home office
- An annual learning and development budget
- Health insurance coverage or a health stipend
- Clear PTO policy

Can you walk me through what's available for remote employees in Vietnam?"

## Remote-Specific Negotiation Scenarios

### Scenario 1: "We adjust for your location"

Company offers $40K for a senior role, citing "Vietnam rates." US equivalent is $120K-$180K.

**Response:** "I understand there's a geographic adjustment. I'd like to understand how the rate was calculated. For context, I've seen comparable remote roles for US companies in Vietnam range from $60K-$100K at this level. Can we discuss what's possible within your budget?"

**Leverage points:** Time zone overlap (if you cover US hours), direct collaboration value, comparable offers from other US companies, and the company's cost savings from remote hiring (no office, no benefits overhead).

### Scenario 2: Equity is US-employees only

The offer includes base + bonus but no equity, while US employees at the same level get RSUs.

**Response:** "I noticed the equity component isn't available for Vietnam-based employees. I understand the regulatory complexity. Could we bridge that gap with additional base or signing bonus to make the total package comparable?"

**Leverage points:** The total comp gap is quantifiable -- calculate what US peers receive in equity and negotiate a portion of that as cash.

### Scenario 3: Contractor classification when you want employee status

The company wants to hire you as a contractor to avoid EOR costs.

**Response:** "I'd prefer an employment arrangement through an EOR for the labor protections and benefits it provides. If the company prefers a contractor arrangement, I'd need to adjust my rate to account for the additional costs I'd bear -- health insurance, no social insurance, no paid leave, and self-employment tax."

**Rule of thumb:** Contractor rate should be 25-40% above the equivalent employee rate to cover the benefits gap and lack of job security.

### Scenario 4: Relocation during employment

You move from Vietnam to another country, and the company wants to adjust your pay.

**Response:** "I'd like to understand the company's policy on location changes for remote employees. Is there a grace period, and how is the new rate determined? I want to make sure we have clarity on this before I sign."

**Best practice:** Negotiate a clause that specifies: (1) rate review is triggered only for moves between major geographic tiers, (2) there's a 90-day grace period after the move, (3) the new rate is based on the company's published formula, not arbitrary.

### Scenario 5: Competing offers in different currencies

You have a $60K USD offer from a US company and a S$80K offer from a Singapore company.

**Normalize to a single currency:**
- US offer: $60,000 USD/year
- SG offer: S$80,000 SGD/year = ~$60,000 USD/year (at 1.33 SGD/USD)
- Now compare: total package, benefits, growth trajectory, time zone, and currency stability

**Response:** "I have two offers I'm considering. The US offer is $60K, and I'm evaluating the Singapore offer at S$80K. I'm very interested in this role -- is there room to get closer to $70K to make this my clear first choice?"

## Quick Checklist: Cross-Border Remote Offer Evaluation

1. **Clarify employment structure.** EOR employee, direct entity employee, or contractor? This determines everything.
2. **Confirm currency and payment terms.** USD or local? Payment method (Wise preferred)? Who absorbs transfer fees?
3. **Calculate total annual comp.** Base x 12 (or x 13 if 13th month) + bonus + signing + allowances + equity (if available).
4. **Apply the benefits gap analysis.** Compare what US employees get vs. what you're offered. The gap is often $10K-$30K/year.
5. **Research comparable offers.** Use Levels.fyi, VietnamDevs, Arc.dev, Wellfound, and peer networks to benchmark your offer.
6. **Assess your leverage.** Do you have competing offers? Time zone overlap value? Unique skills in short supply?
7. **Negotiate.** Start with base, then signing bonus, then allowances. Two rounds is standard; three requires competing leverage.
8. **Get everything in writing.** Contract language around currency, payment terms, benefits, and notice period must be explicit.
9. **Plan for tax.** Understand your Vietnam PIT obligations. If EOR, they handle withholding. If contractor, you handle it yourself.
10. **Factor in 13th month.** If not explicitly offered, negotiate for it -- it's expected in the Vietnam market.

## Framework: Calculating Your Minimum Acceptable Rate

Before entering any negotiation, know your walk-away number. For Vietnam-based remote workers, this calculation is different from US-based workers.

**Formula:**
```
Minimum = Annual living expenses (in USD)
        + Self-funded health insurance ($1,000-$3,000/yr)
        + Self-funded learning ($500-$2,000/yr)
        + Equipment depreciation ($500-$1,000/yr)
        + Retirement savings target (10-15% of gross)
        + Tax buffer (10-20% of gross for Vietnam PIT)
        + Risk buffer for contractor (20-40% of base)
        = Minimum gross annual compensation
```

**Worked example (HCMC, single engineer):**
- Living expenses: $12,000/year ($1,000/month)
- Health insurance: $2,000/year (good international coverage)
- Learning budget: $1,000/year (courses, books)
- Equipment: $800/year (depreciation)
- Retirement savings: $4,500/year (15% of $30K target)
- Vietnam PIT: ~$3,000-$6,000/year depending on bracket
- Risk buffer (if contractor): $7,500 (25% of $30K)
- **Minimum acceptable: ~$30,800/year (employee) or ~$38,300/year (contractor)**

This gives you a defensible floor. Your target should be 30-50% above your minimum.
