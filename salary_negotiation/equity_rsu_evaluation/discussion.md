# Equity & RSU Evaluation

Understanding stock options vs RSUs, vesting schedules, strike prices, dilution, tax implications, and how to put a real dollar value on private company equity.

## Key Concepts

- RSUs (Restricted Stock Units): shares granted that vest over time -- no purchase required, value tied directly to stock price
- Stock Options: right to purchase shares at a fixed strike price -- valuable only if current price exceeds strike
- Vesting schedule: typically 4-year with a 1-year cliff (25% vests at year 1, then monthly or quarterly)
- Cliff: if you leave before the cliff date, you receive zero equity -- this is a significant retention mechanism
- Refresh grants: additional equity granted annually to keep your total equity value from decaying as the initial grant vests
- 83(b) election: allows you to pay tax on options at grant date (when value is near zero) instead of at vesting (when value may be much higher) -- only available for options, not RSUs

## Numbers & Benchmarks

- Typical RSU grant for mid-level at large tech: $80k-$200k/year (total 4-year grant: $320k-$800k)
- Typical RSU grant for senior at large tech: $150k-$400k/year (total 4-year grant: $600k-$1.6M)
- Option pool at startups: typically 10-20% of total shares; individual grants range from 0.05%-2.0% depending on stage and role
- Strike price for options: typically set at the 409A valuation, which is the IRS-mandated fair market value
- Tax on RSU vesting: taxed as ordinary income at vest; you will owe 22-37% federal + state income tax
- Tax on option exercise (ISOs): no regular income tax at exercise if held; AMT may apply; long-term capital gains if held 2 years from grant + 1 year from exercise
- Startup equity success rate: roughly 1 in 10 startups produce meaningful returns for common shareholders; 1 in 100 produce life-changing returns

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating options and RSUs as equivalent -- options can expire worthless while RSUs always have some value
- Not asking about the company's 409A valuation, latest preferred price, and total valuation when evaluating startup equity
- Ignoring dilution -- your percentage ownership decreases with every funding round
- Forgetting that equity is illiquid at private companies -- you cannot sell it until a liquidity event (IPO, acquisition, or secondary sale)
- Not understanding double-trigger acceleration (change of control + termination) vs single-trigger acceleration
- Exercising options without understanding the tax consequences -- AMT can create a massive unexpected tax bill

## Scripts & Talking Points

- "Can you walk me through the equity grant in detail? I'd like to understand the number of shares, current strike price, 409A valuation, and the company's most recent valuation."
- "What is the vesting schedule, and is there a cliff? Also, are there refresh grants, and if so, what does the typical refresh look like for someone at this level?"
- "What happens to my equity in the event of an acquisition? Is there double-trigger acceleration?"
- "For the options, what is the exercise window if I leave the company? Is there early exercise availability, and does the company offer an 83(b) election?"
- "Can you help me understand the dilution outlook? How much of the option pool remains, and are there anticipated funding rounds that could further dilute common shareholders?"
