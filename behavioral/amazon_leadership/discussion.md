# Amazon Leadership Principles: The Complete Interview Playbook

Source: [Amazon Leadership Principles: The Complete Interview Playbook (CalibreOS)](https://www.calibreos.com/learn/craft-amazon-leadership-principles)

## One-Liner
Prepare structured STAR stories mapped to Amazon's 16 Leadership Principles, calibrated to the Bar Raiser scoring rubric, with specific signals for SDE II through Staff/Principal levels.

## How Amazon Behavioral Interviews Actually Work
- Each interviewer is assigned 1-2 LPs to probe; a 5-person loop evaluates 5-10 LPs with intentional overlap
- The Bar Raiser (BR) is structurally separate -- from a different team, holds an effective veto; a BR "No Hire" overrides any number of "Strong Hire" votes
- Scoring is granular on a 1-5 scale per LP: Strong No Hire (1), No Hire (2), Inclined (3), Hire (4), Strong Hire (5)
- You need to clear the threshold on enough LPs without significant red flags on any single one
- LP mapping is explicit in Amazon's job descriptions; senior SDE roles emphasize Ownership, Think Big, Insist on Highest Standards

## What Separates a 4 from a 5 on Any LP
- **Score 3 (Inclined):** demonstrates the LP with a positive story, quantified result, clear personal contribution
- **Score 4 (Hire):** all of the above, plus genuine challenge/adversity, explicit tradeoffs articulated, and a "what I'd do differently" reflection showing self-awareness
- **Score 5 (Strong Hire):** all of the above, plus scope of impact meaningfully beyond the level's typical range, learning demonstrably changed future behavior, interviewer cannot identify a significant gap
- The most common mistake: candidates prepare "positive stories" and score 3s everywhere -- Score 4+ requires real friction and a real lesson

## All 16 Leadership Principles: What Each Actually Tests

### 01. Customer Obsession
- Tests whether you start from customer needs and work backwards, not from technology preference
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you pushed back on a product direction because it wasn't right for the customer."
- Red flag: "customer" means internal stakeholder; Amazon means the external end user
- Senior signal: You collected customer data before the solution was defined, not after

### 02. Ownership
- Tests end-to-end accountability beyond your scope, including owning failures and problems in another team's code
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you took ownership of a problem outside your job description."
- Red flag: Every story involves full authority; Ownership is only visible when you fix things you weren't required to fix
- Senior signal: You owned a production failure that started in another team's code

### 03. Invent and Simplify
- Tests both innovation AND reduction of complexity; most candidates nail "invent" and forget "simplify"
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you invented a solution that also reduced system complexity."
- Red flag: Adding a new system without removing old ones, or innovating without measuring maintenance overhead
- Senior signal: The innovation made the system easier for the next engineer, not just more powerful

### 04. Are Right, A Lot
- Tests judgment quality and intellectual honesty, how you update beliefs when evidence contradicts them
- Key question: "Tell me about a decision you were confident about that turned out to be wrong."
- Red flag: Never being wrong, or blaming incomplete information rather than a reasoning gap
- Senior signal: Changed technical position based on data in a high-stakes situation and can articulate what changed your mind

### 05. Learn and Be Curious
- Tests proactive self-development, what you taught yourself without being asked
- Key question: "Tell me about something complex you taught yourself recently and how you applied it."
- Red flag: Vague answers about "reading blogs"; must name specific resource, skill, and production application
- Senior signal: Self-directed learning directly unblocked a project or changed technical direction

### 06. Hire and Develop the Best
- Tests your bar for hiring and specific investment in others' growth
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you raised the hiring bar by pushing back on a candidate others wanted to hire."
- Senior signal: Specific growth gap identified in a teammate, intervention designed, measurable improvement

### 07. Insist on the Highest Standards
- Tests willingness to reject "good enough" under shipping pressure, with specifics about WHERE you raised the bar
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you insisted on higher quality when the team was willing to accept less."
- Red flag: "Not shipping until it was ready" without specifying what standard you protected and why it mattered
- Senior signal: Name the specific quality dimension, the business cost of not raising it, and the team friction navigated

### 08. Think Big
- Tests strategy over tactics, ability to set a 3-year direction rather than optimize the next sprint
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you proposed a direction significantly more ambitious than what the team was considering."
- Red flag: Large project scoped before you joined; Think Big requires YOU expanded the scope
- Senior signal: Ambitious direction was adopted and is now part of the team/org's multi-year roadmap

### 09. Bias for Action
- Tests calculated risk-taking, acting without full information when delay is costlier than uncertainty
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you took action with incomplete information. What was the risk?"
- Red flag: Mistaking "moving fast" for Bias for Action; must be calculated with a rollback plan
- Senior signal: Explicitly name the cost of NOT acting, which is why you proceeded despite uncertainty

### 10. Frugality
- Tests ability to do more with less, treating resource constraints as forcing functions for creative solutions
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you achieved a significant result without significant resources."
- Red flag: Working extra hours; Frugality means a creative architectural or process solution, not heroics
- Senior signal: Constraint produced a permanently better solution than the unconstrained version would have

### 11. Earn Trust
- Tests candor, willingness to surface bad news, acknowledge mistakes publicly, be diplomatically honest
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you delivered bad news to a senior leader."
- Red flag: Softening a message to avoid conflict; Earn Trust requires saying the hard thing directly
- Senior signal: Proactively surfaced a problem before being asked, even with personal risk

### 12. Dive Deep
- Tests staying connected to details, verifying, auditing, looking at data yourself
- Key question: "Tell me about a time the summary from a team was wrong. How did you discover that?"
- Red flag: Trusting reports/dashboards at face value; Dive Deep requires personal verification
- Senior signal: Pulled logs, ran queries, or audited code yourself and found something important the summary missed

### 13. Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit
- The hardest LP: pushing back with data, losing the argument, then executing fully and enthusiastically
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you strongly disagreed with a decision made above you."
- Red flag: "I convinced them" (not Disagree AND Commit) or privately disagreed but said nothing
- Senior signal: Name the explicit moment you committed; execution of the disagreed decision was indistinguishable from a supported one

### 14. Deliver Results
- Tests achieving outcomes despite obstacles, not just activity or "the team delivered"
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you were behind on a critical deadline. How did you recover?"
- Red flag: Circumstances improved without your intervention, or delivered late without owning the miss
- Senior signal: Name specific obstacles, decisions YOU made to overcome them, and business impact of delivering

### 15. Strive to be Earth's Best Employer
- Tests investment in psychological safety, inclusivity, team environment
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you created an environment where someone felt safe to raise a concern."
- Senior signal: Someone on team surfaced a risk/failure early because of something you did, outcome was better

### 16. Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility
- Tests long-term thinking about broader societal/environmental impact, most relevant at principal level
- Key question: "Tell me about a time you considered the long-term societal impact of a technical decision."
- Senior signal: Advocated for a harder/more expensive approach because of a principle about downstream impact

## The 5 LPs Most Probed at Senior and Staff Level
1. **Ownership (SDE II+)** -- The single most important LP at senior level; ownership is only visible when you go BEYOND your scope
2. **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit (Staff/Principal)** -- Separates mid-level from senior more sharply than any other; requires losing the argument AND committing fully
3. **Think Big (Senior to Principal)** -- The bar rises with level: sprint-sized project (SDE II), multi-quarter direction (Senior), 3-5 year strategy adopted by others (Principal)
4. **Dive Deep (SDE II+, especially EM/TL)** -- "I asked the team to investigate" is delegation, not Dive Deep
5. **Insist on the Highest Standards (SDE II+)** -- Must be a specific story where you stopped shipment; what would have happened to the customer if you hadn't insisted?

## STAR Format at Amazon: The Time-Allocation Rule
| Section | Time Budget | What Interviewers Score | Common Mistake |
|---------|------------|------------------------|---------------|
| Situation | 45-60 seconds (20%) | Stakes established, why did this matter? | 3-minute company history with no stakes |
| Task | 20-30 seconds (10%) | Your specific role, not the team's role | "I was part of the team that worked on..." |
| Action | 90-120 seconds (50%) | Judgment, alternatives rejected, friction navigated, decisions owned | Chronological log of what happened with no reasoning |
| Result | 45-60 seconds (20%) | Quantified business impact + learning + "what I'd do differently" | "The project was successful and leadership was happy" |

Every Action must contain: (a) at least one alternative you considered and rejected with a reason, (b) a moment of friction/pushback you navigated, (c) a decision that was yours to make.

## Five Anti-Patterns That Fail at Amazon
1. **Collective language ("We built," "our team did"):** Amazon scores individually; every decision must have "I" as the subject
2. **Stories within your authority:** Ownership is only visible when you act outside your scope
3. **Positive-only stories:** Stories without adversity score 3 at best; every LP story needs a "could have gone wrong" moment
4. **"I convinced them" as Disagree and Commit:** This LP requires LOST argument and full execution of the opposing decision
5. **Vague learning ("I learned to communicate better"):** Learning must be specific and behavioral

## Disagree and Commit: The LP Most Candidates Fail
Three elements must ALL be present:
1. **Genuine disagreement articulated before the decision** -- you stated your case with data, not just felt uncomfortable
2. **The decision went against you** -- you LOST the argument; "and then they saw I was right" is a different LP
3. **You committed fully and visibly** -- execution of the disagreed decision was indistinguishable from a supported one

The authenticity test: Interviewers probe specifics -- what you said in the kickoff meeting, how you allocated your team's work, how you handled questions from engineers who agreed with your original position.

## Follow-Up Variations
- How would you handle a follow-up question like "What did you say to your team after the decision was announced?"
- What if you cannot think of a genuine Disagree and Commit story -- can you construct one from a smaller disagreement?
- How do you adapt stories when the interviewer probes a different LP than you expected?
- What if the interviewer asks for a second story for the same LP?
