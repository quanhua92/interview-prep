# Initial Offer Evaluation

How to read an offer letter, identify all negotiation levers, and compare multiple offers using a structured scoring framework.

## Key Concepts

- An offer letter is a starting point, not a final statement -- every component is potentially negotiable
- You cannot negotiate what you do not understand -- read the full offer including all attached documents
- Identify your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) before evaluating any offer
- Use a scoring framework to compare offers objectively rather than relying on gut feeling
- Always evaluate the present value of equity, not just the grant size (consider vesting schedule, strike price, and company trajectory)
- The person extending the offer is often not the same person who approves the budget -- understand the chain

## Numbers & Benchmarks

- Typical time to evaluate and respond to an offer: 3-7 business days is standard and reasonable
- First offer is typically 10-20% below the approved budget -- companies expect negotiation
- Candidates who negotiate salary see an average increase of $5k-$15k; those who negotiate multiple components see $20k-$50k+ in additional value
- Offer acceptance rate at most companies: 70-85%, which means they have room to improve terms to win you
- When comparing offers, assign weights: 40% TC, 20% growth/learning, 15% team/culture, 15% WLB, 10% commute/flexibility (adjust to your priorities)
- Always request offers in writing -- verbal offers are not binding and details can shift

## Common Pitfalls

- Accepting the first offer out of excitement or pressure -- always take time to review
- Not reading the equity agreement, which may contain cliffs, clawback provisions, or unusual vesting terms
- Focusing on only one lever (usually base salary) when multiple levers can be adjusted
- Not asking for clarification on vague terms like "competitive bonus" or "equity commensurate with level"
- Failing to consider the cost of benefits differences between companies (premiums, deductibles, OOP maximums)
- Forgetting to factor in commute costs, which can differ by $5k-$15k/year between remote, hybrid, and on-site roles

## Scripts & Talking Points

- "Thank you for the offer. I am excited about the opportunity and want to give it the thoughtful review it deserves. When do you need a response?"
- "Could you send me the full offer package including the equity agreement and benefits summary so I can review everything together?"
- "I have a few questions about the offer. Can you clarify the bonus structure -- is the target guaranteed, and what percentage of employees typically receive the full amount?"
- "How does the equity grant work? What is the current strike price, vesting schedule, and are there any acceleration clauses?"
- "I'd like to discuss the offer further. Based on my research and experience, I was expecting a package closer to $X. Is there flexibility to get there?"

## FAANG Offer Anatomy: Component-by-Component Negotiability

| Component | What It Is | Negotiability | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Base Salary** | W-2 income, taxed at highest marginal rate, fixed monthly | Low -- bands are narrow at senior+ levels (~$10-20K movement typical) | Get clarity on band ceiling first; if at ceiling, redirect to other components |
| **RSU Grant** | Equity in company shares, vested over 4 years | Medium -- grant size has more flex than base at offer stage | Ask for total grant value in dollars; negotiate shares x current price |
| **Signing Bonus** | One-time payment at hire, often partially clawed back if you leave before 1 year | High -- no ongoing cost to company; most negotiable component | Ask for 3-6 months of base; often 50-100% improvable from first offer |
| **Annual Bonus** | Target % of base (10-25% depending on level and company) | Low -- set by level and policy; rarely negotiable at offer stage | Understand target %; it affects 4-yr TC calculation significantly |
| **Refresher Grants** | Additional RSU grants given annually to retain engineers (Meta, Google) | Not directly negotiable at offer -- established post-hire | Factor into company comparison: absent at Amazon, present at Meta/Google |
| **Benefits** | Health, 401K match, FSA, equity buyback programs, etc. | Not negotiable as individual items | Compare total value; Google's 401K match and RSU buyback are significant |

## The 4-Year Total Compensation Formula

Never evaluate an offer by its first-year or annual compensation figure. The components have very different timing, and first-year TC is almost always lower than subsequent years due to cliff structures and delayed RSU vesting.

**4-Year TC = (Base x 4) + Total RSU Vest Value + (Signing Bonus x 1) + (Annual Bonus x 4)**

Apply these adjustments:

- **RSU value:** use current stock price. For private companies (Series B+), apply a 20-30% haircut to the last valuation to account for illiquidity, strike price, and uncertain exit timeline.
- **Signing bonus:** most have a 1-year clawback. If you stay 4 years, you keep it all -- it's a one-time number.
- **Annual bonus:** at FAANG, target is typically paid in full for satisfactory performance. Treat it as likely but not guaranteed.
- **Refresher grants:** at Meta and Google, factor in ~25% of initial RSU grant annually as refresher for years 2-4. Amazon gives no automatic refreshers.

**Worked Example (Meta E5, Bay Area, 2024 typical):**

- Base: $215,000 -> x 4 = $860,000
- RSU grant: $320,000 total over 4 years (vests quarterly: ~$80,000/year)
- Signing: $75,000 (one-time)
- Annual bonus: $215,000 x 20% = $43,000 -> x 4 = $172,000
- Refresher: ~$80,000/year in years 2-4 = ~$240,000 additional
- **Total 4-Year TC: approximately $1,347,000 (~$337K/year average)**

First-year TC on this offer would appear to be ~$338K (including signing). Year 2 drops to ~$263K (no signing). Year 3-4 increase as refreshers compound. Evaluating by first-year TC without understanding refreshers and signing normalization leads to misleading comparisons.

## The 6-Step Offer Evaluation Process

### Step 1: Ask for Time (Always)

Your first response to any offer: "Thank you, I'm excited. Could I have until [date 10-14 days out] to review the full details?" This is universally granted. Never react to an offer in the moment.

### Step 2: Decompose the Offer Into Components

Break the offer into: base salary, RSU total grant and vesting schedule, signing bonus and clawback terms, annual bonus target percentage, and whether refresher grants apply. First offers often blur components to make a number look larger -- signing inflates year 1, back-loaded RSUs understate year 2 income. Calculate your 4-year TC explicitly.

### Step 3: Research Market Rate

Use levels.fyi, Blind, and Glassdoor filtered to your exact level, metro area, and the last 3-6 months. Data older than 6 months may reflect a different market environment. Your research gives you the anchor: "Market for this level in this metro is $X-$Y. My target is $Z."

### Step 4: Assess Your BATNA

- Multiple FAANG offers: maximum leverage.
- One FAANG offer: moderate leverage (you have market data).
- No other offers: low leverage -- use market data and signing bonus focus.

If you can get a competing offer before negotiating, do it. Even 2 more days of delay to get a second offer can change your leverage by $30K+.

### Step 5: Make the Ask, Specifically

"Can you do better?" produces vague improvements. "I'm targeting $340K TC based on market data for this level" produces specific improvements. Name your number, name the source, name the component you want moved.

### Step 6: Negotiate Components Separately

If total TC is stuck, go component by component: base first, then RSU, then signing. If base is at the band ceiling, move to RSU. If RSU is also fixed, signing is almost always the most flexible. Each component has a different approver internally -- exhausting one doesn't mean others are fixed.

## Detailed Negotiation Scripts by Scenario

**The Core Principle: Never give your number first.** If the recruiter asks "What are you looking for?" respond: "I'm really focused on finding the right role. Can you tell me what the total compensation package looks like for this position?" You want to see their number before anchoring yourself.

### Script 1: Competing Offer (strongest leverage)

"I'm genuinely excited about this role, it's exactly the kind of challenge I'm looking for, and [Company] is my first choice. I do have a competing offer from [Other Company] at $[TC] total compensation. Is there any flexibility to get closer to that range? I'd love to be able to make this an easy decision."

### Script 2: Market Data (when no competing offer exists)

"I've done research on levels.fyi and Blind for [Level] roles in [Metro]. The market range for this level appears to be $[range]. My target is $[target]. I'm really interested in this role -- is there flexibility to get closer to that?"

### Script 3: Component-Specific (when base is at band ceiling)

"I understand the base may be at the top of the band for this level. Could we look at the signing bonus or RSU grant instead? An additional $[X] in signing bonus would bridge the gap significantly, and it has no ongoing cost to the company."

### Script 4: Exploding Offer (buying time without hurting relationship)

"I'm very interested and I want to give you a clear yes. I have another process I'm wrapping up -- could I have until [2 weeks from now] to make a fully informed decision? I want this to be a long-term decision, and I think [2 weeks] will let me do that properly."

### Script 5: Round 2 Push (after first counter)

"Thank you for coming back with [counter]. I appreciate the flexibility. I'm still $[gap] away from making this an easy decision. Is there any additional room, even on the signing bonus, that would help close that gap?"

## Using Competing Offers: Leverage, Timing, and Ethics

A competing offer is the single most effective negotiation lever. It converts "I think I'm worth more" (an assertion) into "I have evidence someone else is paying more" (an objective data point).

**When to disclose:** After you have the offer in hand from the company you're negotiating with, not before. Disclosing during the interview process can add pressure but can also cause a company to expedite and close before you have your best leverage.

- **Do:** name the company and the approximate TC. Specificity is more convincing than vagueness.
- **Don't:** fabricate or exaggerate an offer. If the company verifies (unlikely but possible), a fabricated offer can rescind the real offer.

**Leverage tiers:**

- Same tier (FAANG vs. FAANG): significant leverage. A Google L5 offer vs. a Meta E5 offer gives you strong negotiating position at both.
- Lower tier: moderate leverage. A startup offer at a lower TC doesn't create much leverage.
- Multiple offers: maximum leverage. Three FAANG offers creates an auction dynamic.

**Number of rounds:** Two rounds of negotiation is standard. A third round is only sustainable with a specific, credible competing offer. Without a competing offer, a third round risks coming across as haggling, which can damage the relationship with your future manager.

**Key insight:** The best time to negotiate is before you decide, not after. Once you tell a recruiter "I'm going to accept," you have zero leverage. Maintain ambiguity about your decision until you have your final number.

## Six Anti-Patterns That Weaken Your Negotiating Position

1. **Giving your number first.** "I'm looking for $320K" anchors the conversation at your number, which becomes the ceiling you'll be offered. Never go first -- ask to see their number first.
2. **Accepting verbally before negotiating.** "This sounds great, I'll take it" closes the negotiation. Maintain expressed interest without commitment until you've exhausted your negotiation.
3. **Negotiating without specifics.** "Can you do better?" produces "we'll see what we can do." Name a specific number.
4. **Negotiating only base salary.** Base is the hardest component to move at senior+ levels (bands are tight). Signing bonus is the most negotiable. Focus time proportionally.
5. **Missing the deadline to negotiate.** Most FAANG offers have 1-2 week deadlines. You can almost always get 1-2 additional weeks by asking. Use the time to get competing offers if possible.
6. **Taking the offer rescission risk seriously.** Offer rescission due to good-faith negotiation happens in under 0.1% of cases at major tech companies. Companies invest weeks in hiring -- they're not rescinding over a negotiation ask. If a company rescinds because you asked politely for more money, you learned something important about how they treat employees.

## Equity Risk Assessment: Public vs. Late-Stage Private vs. Early Startup

| Dimension | Public Company (FAANG) | Late Private (Series C+, unicorn) | Early Startup (Seed-Series B) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Liquidity** | Immediate, sell after vesting windows | 1-5 year expected to IPO or acquisition | 5-10+ year expected; many never exit |
| **Valuation certainty** | Current stock price, fully observable | Last round valuation; may be inflated (2021 vintage) | 409A valuation; highly uncertain |
| **Haircut to apply** | 0%, use current price directly | 20-35% for illiquidity and exit risk | 50-70%, most startups don't reach favorable exit |
| **Strike price impact** | None, RSUs have no strike price | Minimal if funded post-2023 | Significant, options with high strike from peak rounds may be underwater |
| **Tax treatment** | RSUs taxed as income at vesting; capital gains on appreciation | ISOs may qualify for AMT favorable treatment if early exercise | ISOs, early exercise for QSBS may be optimal |
| **Offer comparison method** | Use current stock price x shares | Last round price x shares x (1 - haircut) | Lottery ticket -- don't rely on equity in 4-yr TC calculation |
| **Refresher grants** | Meta/Google: yes. Amazon: no. Apple: usually yes | Typically yes if healthy trajectory | Rarely -- most startups only grant at hire and promotion |
| **Recommended 4yr TC weight** | 100% of equity at market value | 65-80% of stated equity value | 0-20% of stated equity value for financial planning |

## Location Adjustment and Cost-of-Living Normalization

A $350K TC offer in San Francisco has meaningfully different purchasing power than $350K in Austin, Seattle, or New York. Most FAANG companies pay "local" rates.

**COL index rule of thumb (SF Bay Area = 100 baseline):**

- San Francisco Bay Area: 100 (baseline)
- New York City: ~95 (slightly lower COL-adjusted value vs. raw number)
- Seattle: ~88 (meaningfully better purchasing power at same raw TC)
- Austin: ~72 (significantly better purchasing power)
- Remote (no office): depends on your location; company may apply geographic pay reduction

**Practical calculation:** If you have a $320K Bay Area offer and a $280K Seattle offer, the Seattle offer has *higher* purchasing power: $280K x (100/88) = ~$318K Bay Area-equivalent. The offers are nearly equal in real terms.

**State income tax is significant:** California taxes income at up to 13.3%. Washington has no state income tax. On $200K in base salary, this represents approximately $20K-$26K/year in additional taxes in California vs. Washington. A $320K Bay Area offer and a $300K Seattle offer have equivalent after-tax income -- the Seattle offer is actually financially superior when tax is included.

When comparing offers across geographies, always normalize for COL and state tax before making a financial decision.

## Quick Checklist: What to Do When the Offer Arrives

1. **Ask for time.** "Thank you, I'm excited. Can I have until [date 1-2 weeks out] to review the full details?" Expected and always granted.
2. **Calculate your 4-year TC**, not first-year TC. Include base, RSU at current price, signing (once), bonus (x4), and refreshers where applicable.
3. **Research market rate** on levels.fyi, Blind, and Glassdoor for the same level and metro within the last 3 months.
4. **If TC is 10%+ below market target:** negotiate. Use the competing offer script if you have one; the market data script if you don't.
5. **Negotiate components separately.** If base is maxed, move to signing. If signing is maxed, ask for more RSUs.
6. **Stop at two rounds without a competing offer.** A third ask without new leverage risks the relationship. With a competing offer, a third ask is legitimate.
7. **Get everything in writing.** Verbal offers don't count. Before giving notice, you should have a signed offer letter with: base salary, RSU grant total and schedule, signing bonus amount and clawback terms, start date, and level.
