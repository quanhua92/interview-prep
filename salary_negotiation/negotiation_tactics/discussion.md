# Negotiation Tactics

Proven strategies for anchoring, framing, timing, and communicating effectively throughout the compensation negotiation process.

## Key Concepts

- Anchoring: the first number mentioned sets the range -- whoever anchors first wins, so you should anchor high
- Framing: present your ask in terms of the value you bring, not what you need or deserve
- The pause: after receiving any number, pause and repeat it back -- silence creates pressure and reveals room
- Never accept on the spot -- even if the offer exceeds expectations, say you need time to review
- Get everything in writing; verbal commitments from recruiters are not binding
- Always negotiate with the person who has budget authority, not a middleman

## Numbers & Benchmarks

- Anchor 15-25% above your target so you have room to converge without going below your minimum
- The "yes, and" technique: when they agree to part of your ask, immediately layer on another request
- Average negotiation rounds: 2-4 back-and-forth exchanges before reaching agreement
- Salary.com, Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and Blind report that 70-80% of employers expect candidates to negotiate
- Candidates who negotiate see 7-15% higher starting compensation on average
- The best time to negotiate is after the offer but before signing -- leverage disappears once you accept
- If you have a competing offer, you can typically command 10-25% more than without one

## Common Pitfalls

- Apologizing for negotiating -- "I'm sorry to ask, but..." signals weakness
- Negotiating via email exclusively -- a phone call or video call creates rapport and allows real-time read of reactions
- Making ultimatums unless you are genuinely willing to walk away
- Revealing your minimum acceptable number -- always keep your floor private
- Negotiating every single line item -- focus on the 2-3 levers with the most impact
- Accepting the "we do not negotiate" statement at face value -- push politely with "I understand, but I'd love to explore what flexibility exists"

## Scripts & Talking Points

- "Thank you for sharing that number. I want to make sure I understand -- you are offering $X base, $Y bonus target, and $Z in equity over four years, is that correct?" (pause and let them fill the silence)
- "Based on my 8 years of experience in distributed systems and the scope of this role, I was targeting a base salary in the range of $X to $Y."
- "I appreciate the offer and I am very interested in joining. The base salary is slightly below my target. Could we explore $X, or alternatively, could we look at a larger equity grant or signing bonus?"
- "I have another offer on the table for $X total compensation. I would strongly prefer to join your team. Is there anything we can do to get closer to that number?"
- "I want to be transparent -- my target total compensation is $X. I am flexible on how we structure that between base, bonus, and equity. What options do we have?"
